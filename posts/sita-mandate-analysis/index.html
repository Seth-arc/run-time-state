April 2026 · Governance Note

SITA, Trapped Between Responsibility and Authority.

On South Africa's recurring state IT failures, the FDA–SAPS judgment, and what a reformed SITA mandate would actually need to look like. Developed out of a January 2026 thread by @OfficialSethu.

The Verdict
A structural trap, not rogue behaviour.

SITA is repeatedly asked to guarantee continuity for systems the state depends on, without being empowered to lawfully own, resolve, or terminate them.

FDA–SAPS is not an aberration. It is the predictable endpoint of a mandate designed for procurement, operating in a world that now runs on infrastructure.

01 · Mandate Mismatch

An operator without ownership. SITA hosts, administers and holds privileged access, but does not own the IP, the budget, or the procurement decision.

Consequence
It can keep systems alive technically while losing entitlement legally.
Pattern
Use continues. Authority doesn't.

02 · Infrastructure Blindness

Core state systems are still classified as “procured IT services” even after they become sovereign infrastructure.

Consequence
No doctrine marks the crossing from vendor product to national dependency.
Pattern
Procurement logic fails where continuity logic is needed.

03 · Centralisation Without Tools

Centralisation gives SITA visibility, access and control. It does not give shutdown authority, escrow mandates, or sunset enforcement.

Consequence
SITA inherits risk without instruments.
Pattern
Responsibility arrives; authority stays behind.

04 · Defensive Procurement

In a crisis, SITA retreats to process compliance, “awaiting instructions” and “procurement irregularity.”

Consequence
Individuals are protected; the state's legal position deteriorates.
Pattern
Courts increasingly reject it as a defence.

05 · Incentive Asymmetry

Acting decisively carries personal audit exposure. Delaying diffuses responsibility. The system rewards inaction dressed as compliance.

Consequence
Doing the right thing becomes a career risk.
Pattern
Delay compounds until litigation forces a move.

06 · Replacement Fantasy

“We'll just build another one.” But embedded systems take years to replicate, data gravity is enormous, and users resist.

Consequence
Replacement talk buys time but poisons negotiations.
Pattern
Courts read it as bad faith.

07 · No Stop Rules

No enforced rule says: if a system is mission-critical for X years, the state must own it or escrow it.

Consequence
Disputes drift indefinitely.
Pattern
Resolution only arrives when something collapses.

08 · The Visible Villain

Departments decide. Ministers hesitate. Treasury negotiates. But SITA touches the servers and flips the switches.

Consequence
When it blows up, the law lands on SITA.
Pattern
Structural failure, individual liability.
The Case That Sits Underneath All of This

Before the generalisations, the specific case. The facts below are from a Gauteng High Court judgment I walked through on X in January. The thread is the short-form version; everything else on this page is what follows from it.

I recently read the Gauteng High Court judgement for the case between the SAPS National Commissioner and a company called Forensic Data Analysts. It's a roller-coaster.

Read that sequence twice. Seven years of custom-built mission-critical police infrastructure. A SCOPA intervention. A unilateral decision to stop paying while continuing to use the software. A vendor ignored by both SAPS and SITA when it wrote asking to be paid. A system switched off for non-payment and then switched back on, by SITA, after SITA modified the source code it had no licence to touch. Two court interdicts forbidding exactly that conduct. Continued use in defiance of both. A shadow replacement project running in parallel the whole time. A sheriff dispatched to physically remove the systems. A former minister summoned into a blue-light motorcade. A price agreed at a Presidential lekgotla and reversed inside two hours.

This is not a story about one bad contract or one awkward vendor dispute. It is the organisational reality the About page on the SITA website does not, and structurally cannot, describe. Nothing in the mandate paragraph (consolidate, coordinate, manage the procurement and delivery process, achieve cost savings through scale) gives an employee reading it any instrument for dealing with the situation above. There is no clause that says what to do when a system you do not own has been keeping the police force running for seven years and the paperwork has just quietly stopped matching reality. There is no doctrine for the sheriff at the door. There is no protocol for the blue-light motorcade. And so the sequence that unfolds in the judgment is the sequence an organisation improvises when its published self-description has no language for the situation it is actually in.

How SITA Describes Itself

Read alongside the FDA–SAPS record, SITA's own “About” page reads less like a mandate and more like a document written by an organisation that does not know what it is for.

“SITA's role is to consolidate and coordinate the State's information technology resources to achieve cost savings through scale, increase delivery capabilities and enhance interoperability… managing the IT procurement and delivery process to ensure that the South African government gets value for money, increased productivity; qualitative solutions, services and products; and uses best practice IT approaches in its modernisation and the delivery of services to all citizens.”Source · sita.co.za/page/about
“To be the leading information and communications technology (ICT) agency in public service delivery.”Vision · sita.co.za/page/about

Every verb in that copy belongs to a procurement agent. Consolidate. Coordinate. Manage the procurement and delivery process. Achieve cost savings through scale. None of them describe the stewardship of infrastructure the state cannot turn off. The word ownership does not appear anywhere on the page. Neither does continuity, escrow, sovereignty, lifecycle, sunset, or resolution. The copy is written as if the job ends at the tender award: once the contract is signed and the servers are humming, SITA's work is done and the rest is someone else's problem.

That is not a tone problem or a writing problem. It is a self-understanding problem. An organisation that describes itself only in the language of acquisition will, when dependency crises arrive, reach for the only instruments it believes it possesses: more procurement, more coordination, more delivery management. None of those instruments resolve the FDA–SAPS situation. None of them resolve HANIS. None of them resolve eNaTIS. The vocabulary itself forecloses the possibility of a different kind of action, because there is no word for it in the brochure.

The values published alongside the mandate make the diagnosis sharper, not softer. Each one of them, set against the operational record, reveals a specific way the organisation has quietly stopped being able to recognise itself.

Value 01 · Customer-Centricity
“Exceed customer expectations by providing the best appropriate services and solutions.”

The first problem is that the customer, in SITA's model, is the department. Not the pensioner whose grant did not arrive because CPS and SASSA were still arguing about who had lawful authority to run the payment system. Not the new parent whose child's birth registration is trapped inside a HANIS replacement that has been “nearly ready” for the better part of fifteen years. Not the accused in a criminal matter whose trial is postponed because the digital recording system went down and no one can agree whose contract was supposed to be maintaining it.

Customer-centricity, in a procurement frame, means making the departmental client happy in the moment of transaction. Citizen-centricity, which is the thing the Constitution actually asks for, means being accountable for the continuity of the service the citizen never sees and only notices when it breaks. SITA's copy cannot tell the difference between those two things, and that confusion is not decorative. It is the reason a payment can lapse, a court recording can stall, a forensic system can sit under interdict, and no one inside the organisation feels that the person at the end of the chain is, strictly speaking, their customer at all.

Value 02 · Innovation
“Pursue innovation by demonstrating thought leadership and proactive behaviour on the use of ICT to enhance public service delivery.”

The FDA–SAPS judgment sits at the exact opposite end of the word “innovation” from where SITA's About page places it. What the court describes, and what the thread above walks through tweet by tweet, is an organisation whose instinctive answer, when a dependency becomes uncomfortable, is to modify copyrighted source code it has no licence to touch, switch the system back on in defiance of a vendor cut-off, and then pursue a shadow replacement while denying that the original agreement existed at all. That is not innovation. It is improvisation under legal duress, dressed up as a roadmap, and it ends with a sheriff at the door and a minister in a motorcade.

Real innovation in state IT, in the current moment, would look like things SITA has never publicly proposed. A classification doctrine that distinguishes commodity IT from sovereign infrastructure. A standing escrow regime for any system that crosses the critical threshold. A formal sunset protocol with legal cover for the official who has to sign it. A transition fund. A lifecycle owner. None of these are technically hard. They are institutionally hard, because they require the organisation to stop thinking of itself as a buyer. “Thought leadership,” as a published value, has produced none of them. What it has produced is the word “modernisation” deployed as a negotiating tactic against the vendors of systems the state can no longer operate without.

Value 03 · Integrity
“Conduct our business with integrity at all times to inculcate a culture of honesty, respect and accountability among all our employees.”

There is a specific and uncomfortable sentence in the FDA judgment, and in the judgments that preceded it, where the court makes a finding of mala fides, bad faith, against the state's conduct in the matter. That is not a procedural irregularity finding. It is not “the paperwork was wrong.” It is a judge, looking at the record, and saying that over a period of years the state denied arrangements it knew existed, used software it knew it had no lawful right to run, and reactivated systems that had been placed under interdict. Integrity, in the sense the About page uses the word, is a culture-building aspiration. Integrity, in the sense the court uses the word, is a finding about what actually happened.

A values statement and a judgment cannot both be true in the same way at the same time. The honest thing to do, if SITA believed its own published value, would be to treat the FDA finding as a constitutional event and rewrite the organisation around preventing it. Instead, the more characteristic response has been to argue that this was one unusual case, one unusual vendor, one unusual set of circumstances. It was not. It is the only case in which a vendor had the resources to litigate far enough for the pattern to become visible on the record.

Value 04 · Agility
“Be adaptive and responsive in an evolving environment in order to maintain relevance and competitive advantage.”

eNaTIS has been waiting for a serious replacement conversation since 2007. HANIS has been inside a replacement process whose end date keeps receding since the early 2010s. The PRASA signalling estate degraded visibly for the better part of a decade while the contracts to replace it were fought over. Whatever word one uses to describe an institution whose replacement cycles are measured in decades, “agile” is not it. Neither is “adaptive.” Neither is “responsive.”

What SITA exhibits, in the judicial record, is something closer to the opposite of agility: a reflexive retreat, in every crisis, to the safest possible bureaucratic posture. “Awaiting instructions.” “Procurement irregularity under review.” “The matter is the subject of legal proceedings.” These phrases are not agility. They are the organisational equivalent of freezing. The evolving environment the value statement gestures at is the environment in which embedded systems become national infrastructure within a few years of installation, and no state IT agency in the world can afford to meet that timescale with a decade-long replacement conversation. Relevance and competitive advantage, in that environment, accrue to the institutions willing to own what they run. SITA is not yet one of them.

Value 05 · Collaboration
“Cooperate and support each other in pursuit of our shared goals to achieve synergies and greater productivity.”

The collaboration value, read against the FDA record, is the one that stings. The court record describes a situation in which the state was actively running a proprietary system under a contractual arrangement it was simultaneously denying in writing, while in parallel commissioning work on a replacement intended to displace the same vendor whose software was still keeping the lights on. That is not collaboration. It is, in the plainest possible terms, the opposite of collaboration conducted under the heading of collaboration.

The difficult question the value raises is not whether SITA collaborates with its staff, with its board, or with other departments. It plainly does. The question is whether it can collaborate with the outside parties whose work has become infrastructure for the state, once the commercial relationship becomes inconvenient. The answer, on the record, is that it cannot. And because it cannot, the eventual resolution of every one of these disputes has to arrive through litigation, through Treasury intervention, or through collapse. None of those are collaborative instruments. They are the instruments that activate when collaboration has already failed.

Value 06 · ICT as Game-Changer
“SITA views ICT's as a national socio-economic game changer; through which ICT investment must be equated with growth, impact and citizen satisfaction.”

This is the sentence that most completely gives the game away. Read it carefully. ICT investment must be equated with growth, impact and citizen satisfaction. The unit of analysis is investment: the act of buying. Not operation. Not ownership. Not continuity. The implicit theory of change is that the state procures the right things, and the rest takes care of itself. That is a theory that can only survive in an organisation that has not yet noticed that the hardest part of state IT is what happens after the money has been spent.

If ICT is genuinely a socio-economic game-changer, then the institution responsible for it cannot also treat it as a procurement line item. Those two framings are mutually exclusive. Infrastructure is not purchased once and then managed through a series of vendor contracts; it is inhabited, maintained, evolved, and occasionally rebuilt from the inside by people who understand it well enough to know when to stop paying a vendor and when not to. SITA's own framing, in this one sentence, collapses the category the reform depends on. You cannot be the steward of something you still describe as a purchase. And until the About page is rewritten to describe a different kind of institution, every reform proposal (including the eight in the section below) will land on an organisation that does not yet have the vocabulary to receive them.

The problem is not that SITA fails to live up to its values. The problem is that its values describe a procurement agent, and its job is infrastructure stewardship. The gap between the two is where the unlawfulness accumulates, quietly, continuously, and with the paperwork in order.

The Structural Diagnosis

The FDA record and the eight cases above are symptoms. The pattern repeats not because SITA is incompetent, but because the mandate itself produces the outcomes the mandate claims to prevent. Nine mechanisms hold the dysfunction in place, and together they describe an institution that is structurally successful at the thing it was not supposed to do.

The Core Concept

Rent without performance

Rent, in the economic sense, is income earned not by creating value but by controlling access. Performance is delivery tied to outcomes: uptime, functionality, user value. Rent without performance is the extraction of fees, authority, or control without having to deliver commensurate outcomes.

Departments must route IT procurement through SITA. That control itself becomes the revenue source. SITA earns brokerage fees, hosting charges, and coordination budgets even when delivery is late, degraded, or absent. There is no automatic clawback, no switching option for the customer, and no shutdown option for the vendor. Revenue persists independent of performance.

This is worse than corruption, which is an illegal deviation from the rules. Rent without performance is legal extraction. It survives audits, court cases, and parliamentary outrage because the mandate itself produces the income. Rent without performance is income protected by authority rather than earned through delivery.

01 · Mandate

Mandate schizophrenia

SITA is simultaneously four institutions in one:

  • Regulator (standards, compliance)
  • Broker (procurement middleman)
  • Operator (hosting, networks)
  • Builder (custom systems)

These roles should never coexist inside the same entity. Their coexistence guarantees conflicts of interest and guarantees that no one role can be held to account without the other three being invoked in its defence.

02 · Coercion

Structural coercion, not corruption

Departments cannot bypass SITA, even when SITA lacks the capacity to serve them. That creates forced dependence, and forced dependence creates rent without performance. This is the reason failures repeat without consequences: the customer has nowhere else to go, so the institution has no reason to change.

03 · Payment

Payment discipline broken by design

Systems stay live after contracts lapse. Vendors cannot shut them down without being publicly accused of harming the state. SITA knows this, and extracts value first and regularises later, if ever.

This is not accidental. It is a game-theoretic equilibrium in which the party with the power to stop the music is always the party that will be blamed for the silence.

04 · Procurement

Procurement theatre

Compliance replaces competence. RFPs specify process, not outcomes. Delivery risk is transferred to vendors while control remains with SITA. The predictable result is that the worst vendors survive longest, because survival is rewarded by compliance documentation rather than by outcomes.

05 · Asymmetry

No kill-switch symmetry

In ordinary markets, if the buyer stops paying, the service degrades or stops. In SITA-world, the asymmetry is total:

  • Buyer doesn't pay, service continues.
  • Vendor stops service, vendor is punished.

That asymmetry alone explains roughly 80% of the downstream dysfunction documented in this record.

06 · Insulation

Political insulation

Parliamentary oversight (SCOPA) is retrospective. Executives rotate before consequences land. Operational decisions are diffused across enough desks that there is no single throat to choke. Accountability has to be assembled by an outsider, and the window to assemble it closes faster than the audit cycle.

07 · Capability

Capability decay

Top-tier engineers avoid SITA for three rational reasons: no delivery authority, no technical prestige, and high political risk. Over time, the organisation becomes administrative-heavy and engineering-light, which makes it structurally dependent on the outside vendors it is simultaneously punishing.

08 · Equilibrium

Why this keeps happening

SITA does not fail because it is incompetent. It fails because it is structurally successful at avoiding accountability. The outcomes that look like breakdown from the outside are the outcomes the current mandate produces reliably from the inside.

09 · Diagnosis

One sentence

SITA is a monopoly integrator with no payment enforcement, no delivery liability, and no exit for customers. Every other symptom in this record is a consequence of those three missing constraints.

Condition Market IT SITA IT
Failure to deliver Don't deliver, don't get paid. Don't deliver, stay indispensable.
Customer recourse Lose customers to competitors. Customers trapped inside the mandate.
What earns revenue Uptime and measurable outcomes. The mandate itself. Authority earns the fee.

When access control is guaranteed and exit is forbidden, performance becomes optional. That is the disease.

The systems insight
A Reformed Mandate

The core shift is one sentence: SITA must move from central IT procurement agent to state digital infrastructure steward.

01

Infrastructure Classification Power

Statutory authority to issue a Critical Infrastructure Determination that formally distinguishes commodity IT from national digital infrastructure. The determination triggers mandatory downstream obligations.

02

Mandatory Ownership or Escrow

If the state relies on a system for core sovereign functions beyond a defined threshold, it must either own the IP or hold enforceable source-code escrow with step-in rights, lodged at Treasury.

03

Full Lifecycle Authority

Not just acquisition and hosting, but exit, sunset, buy-out and forced migration. SITA must be able to impose transition deadlines and escalate unresolved systems to Treasury and Cabinet.

04

Emergency Continuity Regime

A formal, time-bound continued-use framework with automatic compensation accrual and transparent reporting. Removes the incentive to deny agreements or invent procurement excuses.

05

Shutdown Authority

Explicit power, with legal protection for the official exercising it, to declare that a system cannot lawfully be used after a specified date unless defined conditions are met.

06

Replacement Realism Rule

Before SITA may endorse or fund a replacement, it must certify realistic timelines, migration feasibility, data portability and overlap plans. No more “we'll build another one” as a tactic.

07

Treasury Resolution Escalator

When a critical system enters frozen-payment, lapsed-contract limbo beyond a fixed window, SITA must trigger a Treasury-led resolution: binding valuation, exit or buy-out, ring-fenced budget.

08

New Performance Metrics

Stop counting tenders processed. Start measuring the percentage of critical systems with clear ownership, time spent in transition, and the count of systems operating post-contract.

Procurement → Infrastructure → Sovereignty. Each phase has different rules. SITA currently pretends they are the same.
