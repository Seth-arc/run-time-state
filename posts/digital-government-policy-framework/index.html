Culture, Coordination, and Capability in South Africa's Draft Digital Government Policy Framework

The Verdict
Technically Sound, Culturally Thin

A capable technical blueprint missing the cultural and behavioural scaffolding digital transformation actually requires.

The Draft Digital Government Policy Framework (DGPF) advances a credible technical vision for modernising South Africa's public service. It names the right ambitions (citizen-centric delivery, interoperability, and Batho Pele alignment) and gestures toward the skills agenda needed to realise them.

However, the framework treats digital transformation primarily as a technology adoption problem rather than a cultural transformation problem. Drawing on Schein's levels of culture and lessons from Estonia and South African universities, four structural gaps emerge: weak emphasis on organisational culture, insufficient national-level coordination, a narrow skills agenda, and the absence of structured implementation and evaluation guidelines.

The result is a document that can be implemented on paper but risks stalling in practice, unless the underlying assumptions, values of integration, and values of adaptation are surfaced and worked on deliberately.

Analytical Framework

A Blueprint for Cultural Transformation

The DGPF is strong on technical ambition but thin on the cultural scaffolding digital transformation needs.

The following four pillars outline my recommended framework for how South Africa must translate global ideals into actionable policies across National, Provincial, and Local tiers.

01. Cultural Transformation

Digital transformation is first a culture problem. The DGPF names outdated processes and leadership gaps but stops short of treating culture and behaviour as a core pillar. Schein's levels (assumptions, espoused values, and observable behaviours) must be made legible and actively managed.

National
Mandate the National School of Government to develop an ongoing National Learning Agenda for digital culture change.
Departmental
Require organisational culture assessments before and during digital transformation initiatives.
Individual
Embed digital orientation into onboarding and core public service induction.

02. National Coordination

Section 11.2.3 gestures at coordination without the machinery to deliver it. Estonia's e-University experience shows that coherent digital transformation needs a unified coordinating body with real authority, not advisory committees layered on existing silos.

National
Establish a National Digital Governance Council to harmonise initiatives across departments.
Departmental
Run regular NSG-led Digital Maturity Assessments to track alignment and readiness.
Workforce
Integrate digital readiness criteria into the Public Service Human Resource Development Strategy.

03. Comprehensive Skills

Section 11.2.10 acknowledges the need for digital skills but lacks the mechanisms to reach all staff. Research in South African universities found the majority of employees are never directly targeted by supposedly institution-wide interventions. The same risk applies here.

Assessment
Regular, anonymised Digital Literacy Assessments aligned to a Digital Competency Framework.
Initiative
A National Digital Skills Initiative for public servants at every level.
Leadership
Dedicated Digital Transformation Leadership Programmes for senior officials.

04. Implementation & Evaluation

Sections 7 and 8 set ambitious objectives and outcomes without the implementation roadmaps or evaluation frameworks needed to reach them. Without structured review, the DGPF risks becoming a statement of intent rather than a driver of change.

Framework
Mandate a comprehensive, data-driven Digital Governance Monitoring & Evaluation framework.
Review
Regular, structured progress reviews channelled through the National Digital Governance Council.
Learning
Feedback loops that convert evaluation findings into revisions of the Learning Agenda.

Final Synthesis

Technically Ready, Culturally Unprepared

The Draft Digital Government Policy Framework reads as a technically competent modernisation plan. It names the right ambitions and anchors itself in Batho Pele. What it does not yet do is treat digital transformation as the cultural project it is: a shift in underlying assumptions about how the state works, whom it serves, and what it rewards.

Estonia's coordinated national approach and the South African university experience both point in the same direction: technology adoption without cultural alignment stalls. The four guiding principles proposed here (cultural transformation, national coordination, comprehensive skills, and structured implementation & evaluation) are not additions to the DGPF. They are the conditions under which it can actually succeed.

The Missing Layer in South Africa's Digital Government Plan

When a government publishes a digital transformation framework, it is tempting to read it as a technology document. Cloud here, interoperability there, a section on skills, a mention of leadership. The Draft Digital Government Policy Framework follows that familiar shape, and on its own terms it is a credible piece of work. But the more time you spend with it, the more you notice what is not on the page.

Digital transformation is not really about technology. It is about the assumptions people carry to work, the behaviours their managers reward, and the stories an institution tells itself about why it exists. Edgar Schein called these the underlying assumptions, the espoused values, and the observable artefacts of organisational culture. You can replace every server in the building and change none of them. If you do, nothing meaningful will change.

This is the missing layer in the DGPF. The framework gestures at culture in its problem statement, noting outdated processes and insufficient leadership involvement, but it treats these as obstacles to be cleared rather than as the substance of the work itself. Research in South African universities suggests why that matters. When institutions announce digital transformation without clearly articulating what it means beyond the core business, and without engaging all staff, uptake stays shallow. The tools arrive. The culture does not move.

The comment I submitted proposes four guiding principles to address this gap. The first is cultural transformation itself, embedded as a principle rather than assumed as a by-product. That means giving the National School of Government a real mandate to run an ongoing National Learning Agenda, and requiring departments to assess their culture before and during digital initiatives, not after they fail.

The second is national coordination. Estonia's success in digital government was not an accident of small size. It came from a unified coordinating structure with genuine authority. Section 11.2.3 of the DGPF gestures at coordination without the teeth to deliver it. A National Digital Governance Council, paired with regular Digital Maturity Assessments across departments, would turn an aspiration into a system.

The third is comprehensive skills development. The DGPF acknowledges the skills problem in Section 11.2.10, but the remedy needs to reach every level of the public service, not only the officials who already sit closest to the technology. Anonymised literacy assessments, a national skills initiative, and dedicated leadership programmes for senior officials would begin to close the gap.

The fourth is structured implementation and evaluation. Objectives and expected outcomes are set out in Sections 7 and 8, but without implementation roadmaps and a monitoring and evaluation framework, they remain ambitions. A data-driven evaluation framework, reviewed regularly through the proposed Council, turns the document into a living instrument.

None of these recommendations argue against the technical work in the DGPF. They argue that the technical work will not travel on its own. Without cultural transformation, coordination, skills, and evaluation, the framework risks becoming another well-drafted document that changes the language public servants use without changing the work they do. With those four layers in place, it can become something rarer: a policy that moves an institution.
